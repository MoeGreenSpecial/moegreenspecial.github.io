Kena: Bridge of Spirits is a beautiful, tightly designed action game that is an impressive debut for first time indie developers Ember Lab.

The Rot, and their hats, that you collect during the game in particular feel right out of something that Pixar might create.  Their cuteness and the overall aesthetic of Kena: Bridge of Spirits belies the game's dark themes.  Visually, it seems like something you could play with a child.  However, Kena can be a difficult game and thematically deals with heavier topics such as death, grief, and the destruction of the place you live.

Working as a small indie studio, Ember Lab made the smart choice to keep the gameplay focused. The world map feels larger than it is, mostly because it is very dense, albeit with an interesting variety of environments throughout the game.  Additionally, Kena: Bridge of Spirits makes good use of Metroidvania style map-gating, locking off certain areas until you've unlocked the ability needed to access them.

Level design in particular is a highlight.  Rarely is there just a linear path to follow, even though much of the game does boil down to following linear quests out of more open hub areas.  Instead, Kena relies on a set of traversal puzzles that are not overly complex, but do keep moving around the world interesting.

Most of the gameplay is standard stuff, get a mission, explore a bit, gather some collectibles, with the occasional puzzle or combat encounter thrown in.  The puzzles weren't very challenging, and were somewhat repetitive, but were generally fine.  The combat felt solid, with new enemy types gradually introduced to keep things from getting stale.

I found the healing system very clever.  Combat arenas frequently contain flowers that you can use to heal.  The tradeoff is that using a healing flower costs a Rot Action, which is the same resource used for your most powerful attacks.  Frequently, I had to make a trade-off between healing up or saving my action for one final powerful attack to end the fight.

Platforming didn't feel great to me, controlling Kena in the air always felt a bit awkward.  There's also a mechanic later in the game where some of the platforms you need to jump on are on a timer and will eventually fall away.  This, combined with the feel of jumping, made for a handful of frustrating platforming sections.  That being said, I never stopped finding it funny when I would miss a jump, the timer would expire, and I would die because one of the platforms literally fell onto my head.

Most of the game focuses on two core stories, and as you work through those stories you'll be removing the corruption from areas of the map. Restoring nature to the map feels very satisfying, but you also find collectibles, called Soul Mail, which enable you to restore the village in the center of the world.  The act of cleaning up the village as you're playing is very satisfying and one of the highlights of the game for me.

Another highlight is a memorable final boss fight.  There are multiple phases to this fight, and you'll wind up using almost all the tools and abilities that you've picked up across the game.

Kena: Bridge of Spirits isn't breaking new ground, but it does what it sets out to do very well.  The artwork is amazing, the level design is good, and restoring the world as you progress is a satisfying through line.  The scope of the game was just right at 10 hours long, and it is an impressive debut game for Ember Lab.
