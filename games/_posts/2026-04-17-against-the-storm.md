Against the Storm is a unique blend of roguelite and city building which nails what it sets out to do, although I found its progression system to be a bit lacking.

In Against the Storm, you are working on building a sequence of settlements, each stretching further from the Smoldering City.  What's unique here is that each settlement only takes a couple of hours to play.  As opposed to a traditional city builder, where once the city gets up and running you need to figure out goals and objectives for yourself, in Against the Storm you merely need to score enough points to win the settlement.

This means you never get into the phase of city building where the city is already built, and you're mostly just doing small refinements. The trade-off is that you're doing the early game portion repeatedly.  Despite the fact that Against the Storm tries to keep you on your toes by varying the buildings, settler abilities, bonuses, objectives, and environmental resources in each settlement, they eventually got a bit same-y for me.

As you're trying to build your settlement, the Queen's Impatience is building.  As bad things happen, the queen gets more impatient.  If you're not constantly making progress toward scoring points, eventually the impatience meter will fill up and the run will end.

Success in Against the Storm is about optimizing production pipelines.  Figure out what items would make the inhabitants of your settlement most happy, and compare that with what you're capable of producing given the resources in the environment and the buildings you have access to.  And then, chain together a sequence of buildings so that you're refining raw materials into the ingredients you need to finally build the products your inhabitants want.

These mechanics prove to be very satisfying in practice.  I constantly felt behind the 8-ball at the start of the game, my score not moving forward very much while the Queen's Impatience steadily grew.  But, eventually, after getting my settlement rolling, and a production pipeline going, I felt like success would be inevitable.

The roguelite elements come into play when you eventually reach a Seal or fail in a settlement.  You can then trade in any Citadel Resources you've gathered to gain permanent upgrades that you use on future runs.  Unfortunately, each of these upgrades felt small and incremental to me.  I never felt that significant burst of power after getting an upgrade that can make the best games in the genre feel so good to play.

I also ran into some technical issues with the game.  Against the Storm does not allow the use of a controller on the PC version of the game, which makes it unplayable on handheld PCs.  It also doesn't support cross-save between the Xbox PC version and the Xbox version, so I couldn't use the Xbox Game Streaming version to play on the handheld either.

The concept of Against the Storm is great, although in practice the settlement building did get to be repetitive.  If it were better playable on handheld, it would absolutely be one that I dipped into and out of.  Unfortunately, it felt a little too repetitive to be the type of game that I'm going to want to play as my primary game for a long stretch of time, so I've put it on hold for now.
