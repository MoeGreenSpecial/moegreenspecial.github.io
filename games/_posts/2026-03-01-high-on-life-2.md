Very similar to the first High on Life, High on Life 2 is a good time, but not a stand-out experience for me.  It's a bright and colorful first-person shooter with hit or miss comedy and excellent movement mechanics that doesn't overstay its welcome.  Impressively for a game of this scope, each level is built around a unique concept, frequently with design, art, and characters that are not reused elsewhere in the game.  In one level you might be moving around a low-poly world being decried as a smooth-skin, while in another you'll find yourself on a sprawling ranch in Montana.  That amount of unique work for each of the main levels is impressive and not something I was expecting from High on Life 2 going in.  Combined with the 15-hour runtime, this really helps keep the experience from feeling repetitive as the story unfolds.

One major issue, however, is that you can’t revisit levels after completing them.  This means that any collectibles and achievements that you miss on your run through a story level are permanently gone for your playthrough.  There are no manual save slots, just an auto save that the game triggers for you, so you can't even load an earlier save and attempt to gather something that you've missed.

The hub area between levels is fairly substantial, although I didn't find it incredibly compelling.  The lack of a mini-map, and side quests such as helping the taxi driver are not marked out, making it so that you're never really sure what there is to do in the world.  Mostly I just used this area as a way to travel between the main missions of the game and didn't spend much time engaging with it.

Traversal in High on Life 2 is one of its absolute highlights.  Once you get the skateboard fairly early in the game, navigating around the world becomes super fun.  Both the hub area and most of the game's levels are set up like giant skateboard parks. You’re constantly flying forward, chaining jumps and grinding from rail to rail. With forgiving, fun-focused physics, a grapple to recover missed rails, and a handful of movement upgrades, simply getting around the map consistently feels great.

Unfortunately, the shooting doesn’t hit the same highs.  The guns don't feel particularly great, and I never felt incredibly compelled to use more than one or two of them.  Once I had access to Gus (the shotgun) and Sheath (the assault rifle), I generally didn't use any of the others unless they were needed to solve a specific puzzle.  Each gun has access to a special secondary mechanic, such as the ability to create time-slowing bubbles, which are used both to solve puzzles and gating progression.  However, the puzzles are very light as each gun effectively has a 1:1 match for the type of puzzle it can solve.  Red splotch on the wall that's too high to climb?  Gus puts a disc there.  Set of electrical connection points?  Sheath can connect them to power something up.  The solutions are rarely more complicated than that.

Most of the time, I chose my active gun based on which character’s commentary I wanted to hear. Each weapon has a distinct personality, and some even get small character arcs. I kept Gus equipped frequently just to enjoy J. B. Smoove’s performance. Still, when certain moments suited another character better, I’d switch so they could react on-screen rather than chime in from off-camera.

The final boss fight uses some clever tricks to force you to go through and use each of the different guns and their special abilities.  I found this fight to be clever and a good capstone for the game, if not just a bit too long.

Much like the first game, the comedy in High on Life 2 is hit or miss.  I'm sure different parts will work for different people.  I personally found Jorb to be incredibly grating and annoying, while I found bits like the Dr. Frasier Crane Game quite funny.  Some of the meta-humor also worked well for me, especially (without spoilers) "The Good Ending" and "Ankhs for the Memories" achievements. 

I played High on Life 2 on PC, and the game was fairly buggy there for me.  It froze or crashed five or six times across my 15 hours of playing, including one final crash after I finished the final portion of the game while it was telling me that I could now go clean up anything I missed in the hub area.  My final interaction with the game was clicking on the fatal error dialog box, which was somewhat fitting considering how frequently I ran into technical issues.

I feel about High on Life 2 much like I did about the original.  The traversal is excellent, the level variety is impressive, and the colorful graphics style is very appealing. But the shooting lacks impact, the puzzles are overly simplistic, and the comedy still swings wildly between clever and grating. It’s a solid sequel, but I don't feel any particular need to pick it back up now that I've rolled the credits.
